About Glasgow

Glasgow, situated along both banks of the River Clyde in west-central Scotland, lies approximately 20 miles (32 km) from the river’s mouth on the western coast. As Scotland’s largest city, it forms an independent council area entirely within the historic county of Lanarkshire. The city occupies much of the lower Clyde valley, with its suburbs extending into surrounding districts. Glasgow’s Gaelic name, “Glaschu”, translates to “Green Glen.” Although evidence of a fortified prehistoric village exists on the site, Glasgow truly began to develop around 550 CE with the arrival of St. Kentigern (Mungo), who established a religious community there. The present Glasgow Cathedral, dedicated to St. Mungo and begun in the 12th century, stands on the site of the original chapel. Over time, Glasgow thrived as a market center due to its strategic location between Highland and Lowland Scotland, as well as its proximity to Edinburgh and the west. The city’s fortunes grew significantly after the union of the Scottish and English crowns in 1603. By the 18th century, trade with the Americas—exporting coal, plaid (wool cloth), herring, and later tropical produce like tobacco, sugar, and rum—brought prosperity to Glasgow’s merchants. Today, Glasgow continues to surprise and delight visitors with its colorful character, industrial history, incredible art, and vibrant music scene.
