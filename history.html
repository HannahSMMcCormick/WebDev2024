History of Glasgow

The founding of Glasgow

Glasgow, a city steeped in history, traces its origins to ancient times. Around the 6th century CE, St. Mungo (also known as St. Kentigern) played a pivotal role in shaping the city’s destiny. He established a church at a place called Glas Gu, which translates to “Green Place.” Over time, this humble fishing settlement grew into a small town. The present site of Glasgow sits along the River Clyde, where it served as a vital fording point at the confluence with the Molendinar Burn. The Romans left their mark here, constructing outposts and the Antonine Wall to separate Roman Britannia from Celtic and Pictish Caledonia. After the Romans withdrew, Glasgow became part of the Kingdom of Strathclyde. However, it was during the medieval period that Glasgow truly blossomed. Reorganized by King David I of Scotland and Bishop John, the city’s bishopric gained prominence. By the 12th century, Glasgow had earned the status of a city, and its magnificent Glasgow Cathedral became the seat of bishops and archbishops. The University of Glasgow, founded in 1451, further solidified its reputation as an academic hub.

The Victorian age in Glasgow

During the Victorian era, Glasgow underwent a remarkable transformation. As the Industrial Revolution took hold in the early 19th century, the city became a bustling hub of industry. Cotton mills, textile factories, chemical plants, glassworks, and soap production flourished along the banks of the River Clyde. Immigrants from the Highlands and later from Ireland formed the workforce, propelling Glasgow’s population from a quarter of a million to a staggering 762,000 by the end of Queen Victoria’s reign. The cotton industry alone employed nearly a third of the workforce at its peak. Glasgow diversified further into heavy industries such as shipbuilding and locomotive construction, fueled by abundant coal and iron ore supplies. Between 1870 and 1914, Glasgow stood as one of Europe’s wealthiest and most vibrant cities. However, alongside prosperity, the city grappled with social challenges—poverty, crime, and disease—and stark wealth inequality. The splendid mansions in the West End contrasted sharply with the crowded wynds and closes of the East End. Glasgow’s commitment to municipal services, slum clearance, and public welfare gradually improved living conditions. The city’s pride in its achievements was showcased through grand exhibitions, and Glasgow rightfully earned its title as the “Second City of the Empire”. Notably, landmarks like the Kibble Palace in the Botanic Gardens and the iconic Kelvingrove Art Gallery & Museum epitomized Victorian Glasgow’s cultural and architectural vibrancy

Cone Man

The equestrian statue of Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington, stands proudly outside the Gallery of Modern Art in Glasgow, Scotland. Sculpted by Italian artist Carlo Marochetti, this iconic landmark was erected in 1844 to commemorate the successful end of the French Revolutionary Wars and the Napoleonic Wars in 1815. However, what truly captures the imagination is not the Duke himself but his beloved hat—an orange traffic cone. Since at least the 1980s, mischievous passersby have playfully adorned the Duke’s head with this unconventional headgear. The act of placing a traffic cone on the statue has become a cherished tradition, symbolizing the irreverent humor of the local population. Over the years, the cone has become an integral part of Glasgow’s identity, reflecting the city’s spirit and resilience. In 2005, Glasgow City Council and Strathclyde Police discouraged the practice, citing minor damage to the statue and safety concerns. Yet, the tradition persisted. In fact, the cone-topped Duke even made it to the Lonely Planet’s list of the “top 10 most bizarre monuments on Earth” in 2011. Efforts to remove the cones were met with public opposition, including an online petition with over 10,000 signatures. The statue’s plinth was proposed to be raised to deter vandals, but the plans were eventually withdrawn. Instead, the cone remained—a testament to Glaswegian wit and defiance. The Duke’s hat has seen various transformations: a gold-painted cone during the 2012 Olympics, a “Yes” cone in support of the Scottish Independence referendum, and even a flag in the stirrup. It has become a symbol of Glasgow’s creativity, resilience, and unwavering spirit
